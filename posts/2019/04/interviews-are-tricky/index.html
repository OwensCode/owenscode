Interviews Are Tricky

April 8, 2019

I had an interview with a potential new client last week and was reminded how tricky interviews can be, especially if they are infrequent. My last interview was about 3 years ago, and was not very technical or demanding. I feel a little bit like I got to lean on the reputation of my company, which isn’t a great feeling. I prefer to stand on my own and lead from the front, and this time I’m not sure I would’ve gotten past an initial phone screening if I was interviewing directly for a new position.

On the other hand, I probably would’ve had more than 30 minutes to prove myself over a staticky line, and hopefully would not be squeezing the interview in between trying to learn something totally unrelated for my current project. I had spent a few hours over the weekend “studying” to remind myself about some Scala syntax and clarifying some functional programming concepts so that I could speak about them intelligently instead of just having a vague (but possibly usable) outline of them in my head. It was a bit of stretch to get my mind to refocus on Python, Airflow and Talend for a few hours in the morning, and then jump back into Scala, Functional Programming, and Reactive Programming for the interview.

My main frustration with myself was that I was asked about how I had handled concurrency in the past and I totally fumbled it. I think I was so focused on the fact that I am not an experienced functional programmer and don’t have a strong understanding of how it would be handled in Akka, although I know that is one of the things that an actor based system promises to make easy, that I forgot about how I had handled it in object oriented systems, and how in one system in particular I had used some functional concepts to handle concurrency pretty cleanly. I was not expected to be a functional programming expert, and here I had missed an opportunity to showcase some relevant past experience.

I’m still trying to stop kicking myself and taking it as a learning experience. I’m wondering if there is a good way to get some interview practice without actually going for job interviews. Aside from the time taken away from work and family, I would feel a little dishonest interviewing for jobs that I don’t intend to take. Still, I have heard others recommending it as a way to keep your resume up to date, keep you in good practice for when you need it, and generally just improve your skills at selling yourself, which is important even within your own company.
